IV: Marche au Supplice - March to the Scaffold

Symphonie Fantastique, Hector Berlioz

The Distant Parade

It is easier to hear drums than other instruments, so often in a parade, the drums will begin playing first, and the brass and wind instruments will join in when the procession is much closer to the crowds.

The March of the Condemned: intro

The movement begins with the March of the Condemned melody played softly, and gradually louder, as the young artist begins his journey to the scaffold, and comes to terms with his fate.

Les Francs-Juges

From 1825 to 1826, Berlioz worked on his first opera, Les Francs-Juges, or The Judges of the Secret Court. Although he abandoned the opera without finishing it, he would later reuse parts of it in this movement of Symphonie Fantastique.

The March of the Condemned: 1st repeat

The theme repeats for the second time

The March of the Condemned: 2nd repeat

Once again, the theme repeats, and we hear it for a third time

The March of the Condemned: 3rd repeat

This theme repeats for the fourth time. Slightly longer than the previous iterations, this section slowly transitions to the next main melody of this movement.

The Military March: introduction

Berlioz grew up in post-Napoleonic France, and this movement, especially the military march melody, is heavily influenced by the military band music that became popular under Napoleons rule. Other historical influences for this movement include the French Revolution.

The Military March: 2nd repeat

The parade theme repeats for the second time.

The Return of
the Condemned

Amidst the sounds of the military march, the march of the condemned returns, but this time as a klangfarbenmelodie.

Klangfarbenmelodie

A klangfarbenmelodie, translating roughly to sound-color melody is a technique where the musical line of a melody is split between multiple instruments. Here, the march of the condemned can be heard as a klangfarbenmelodie, with one section of instruments taking over a few notes of the melody before passing it on to a different section, and so on, between the strings and the winds.

The Military March:
a repeat of before

Despite the brief glimpse into the artist's plight, the parade is ongoing, and the military march returns once again. Just like before, the march is played twice, followed by a segment of the condemned's theme.

The Military March:
2nd repeat

The parade theme repeats for the
second time.

The Condemned
Returns Again

Like before, we hear a short segment of the march of the condemned as a klangfarbenmelodie, where different notes of the melody are played by different sections of the orchestra

The Journey Continues

The scaffold looms as the procession slowly approaches

The Condemned

As the artist finally comes to terms with his fate, the march of the condemned loudly and chaotically bursts forth.

The Execution

The condemned artist is forced to the scaffold. A descending melody in the strings can be heard as he kneels.

The Ide Fixe Returns

Amidst the jeering mob of the brass instruments, a clarinet breaks through the noise with a final, brief glimpse of
the ide fixe.

A Final Fanfare

The guillotine comes crashing down.
With two plucked notes in the cellos and basses, the artists decapitated head bounces into the basket, and a final
fanfare plays.

Symphonie Fantastique

Hector Berlioz's first, a story of unrequited love and despair, is Berliozs love letter to Shakespeare, Beethoven, Opera, and himself, and in turn revolutionized telling stories through music, and in particular, program music. Come explore the fourth movement, March to the Scaffold!

Having become certain that his love goes unrecognized, the artist poisons himself with opium. The dose of the narcotic, too weak to kill him, plunges him into a sleep accompanied by the most horrible visions. He dreams that he has killed the woman he had loved, that he is condemned, led to the scaffold, and that he is witnessing HIS OWN EXECUTION."
-from Berlioz's Program Notes
